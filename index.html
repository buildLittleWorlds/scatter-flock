A paper you can poke

Nobody is in charge of the murmuration

A thousand starlings, no choreographer. Three little local rules—keep apart, line up, stay close—and the sky gets a dance nobody designed.

DO THISMove the three sliders until the flock looks most alive—then break it and rebuild it. There’s no correct setting. You’re tuning, not solving.

What to notice

No single dot leads. A “leader” at the front is just whichever dot happens to be there this second—a moment later it’s buried in the middle. The shape you see isn’t stored anywhere; it’s re-computed from scratch every frame out of neighbors reacting to neighbors.

What this really shows vs. only suggests

Really shows: local rules with no central plan can produce coordinated global order. That much is genuine—every dot here only ever looks at the handful of others near it, and the flock is real emergence, not a scripted animation.

Only suggests: that this is how starlings do it. Real birds track a fixed number of nearest neighbors (about seven) rather than everything inside a radius, and a lot of a murmuration’s drama is predator-dodging, not just the three rules. This is a cartoon of the idea, not a model of the animal.

The hint worth keeping: the same trick—order out of nobody-in-charge—shows up far from birds. Traffic jams form and dissolve with no lead car. Applause in a hall syncs with no conductor. A crowd leaving a stadium threads the exits with no floor plan. Whenever a big smooth pattern appears and you go looking for who’s running it, it’s worth asking whether anyone is—or whether it’s just neighbors, reacting to neighbors, all the way down.
